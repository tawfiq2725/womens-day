About Malala

A Voice for Education

Born on July 12, 1997, in Mingora, Pakistan, Malala Yousafzai grew up in a region where the Taliban had banned girls from attending school. Despite the dangers, Malala spoke out publicly against the prohibition and continued to attend school.

In 2012, at the age of 15, she survived an assassination attempt when a Taliban gunman shot her in the head while she was returning home from school. Following her recovery, Malala became a global advocate for the millions of girls being denied a formal education because of social, economic, legal and political factors.

In 2014, at age 17, she became the youngest person ever to receive the Nobel Peace Prize. Today, through her Malala Fund, she continues to be a leading advocate for girls' education and women's equality.

Malala's Journey

1997

Birth in Mingora

Born on July 12, 1997, in Mingora, Swat Valley, Pakistan, to Ziauddin and Toor Pekai Yousafzai.

2009

BBC Blog

At age 11, Malala began writing an anonymous blog for the BBC about life under Taliban rule and their ban on girls' education.

2012

Assassination Attempt

On October 9, 2012, a Taliban gunman shot Malala while she was returning home from school, severely wounding her.

2013

United Nations Speech

On her 16th birthday, Malala delivered a powerful speech at the UN, calling for worldwide access to education.

2014

Nobel Peace Prize

At age 17, Malala became the youngest person to receive the Nobel Peace Prize for her struggle against the suppression of children and young people.

2018

Return to Pakistan

Malala returned to Pakistan for the first time since the attack, visiting her hometown in Swat Valley.

The Malala Fund

Breaking Barriers to Girls' Education

The Malala Fund is working for a world where every girl can learn and lead without fear. Founded by Malala and her father Ziauddin in 2013, the organization champions every girl's right to 12 years of free, safe, quality education.

The Fund invests in education advocates and activists who are challenging the policies and practices that prevent girls from going to school in their communities.

Education Champions

Supporting local educators and advocates who understand the challenges in their communities.

Advocacy

Amplifying girls' voices and advocating for policy changes at local, national and international levels.

Emergency Response

Providing resources and support during crises when girls' education is threatened.

Global Reach

Working in regions where girls face the greatest barriers to education, including Afghanistan, Brazil, India, Nigeria, Pakistan and Syria.
